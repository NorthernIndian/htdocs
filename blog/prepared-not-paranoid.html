Prepared; Not Paranoid

The Night the Lights Went Out

Imagine you're at home. It’s a normal weeknight. You’re cooking dinner, maybe yelling at the kids, and you finally sit down to relax. It’s one of those evenings where everything feels routine… almost boring.

Then the power goes out.

You check your phone — no reception. You look outside — pitch black. Streetlights, houses, everything. You shrug and grab a couple extra blankets because it's winter, and houses cool down fast without a furnace.

Morning comes. Still no power. You decide to drive to work. You get in your vehicle, turn on the radio — static. Every traffic light is out. Cars are gridlocked. Nobody knows what’s going on. You turn around and go home.

A week later, there’s still no power. No communication. Your home is cold. Your pantry is empty.

What do you do?

The Movie That Sparked It

A few weeks ago, my wife and I randomly put on a film called Leave the World Behind. It’s not your typical apocalypse movie. No aliens. No zombies. No asteroids. Just one thing: the world goes dark… and never turns back on.

And for some reason, that scenario freaked me out more than any other disaster movie ever has. It made we wonder:

What if the power went out in January?

What if trucks stopped delivering food?

What if the water from the tap simply stopped flowing?

It was scary to think about, especially with two young kids. Sure, I can keep them warm with blankets and live off noodles for a couple days, but after that, there’s no plan.

Out of curiosity, and a strange morbid fascination, I went down a rabbit hole to find the most likely “Modern Apocalypse” scenarios.

The Most Likely “Modern Apocalypse” Scenarios

1. Solar Flares (Carrington Event)

The Sun regularly sends out massive bursts of energy called solar flares. More than 99% of the time, that energy either misses Earth or dissipates harmlessly. But occasionally, Earth is hit — creating what’s known as a Carrington event.

The last major one hit Earth in 1859 and was powerful enough to fry telegraph systems and cause electrical fires.

If a Carrington-level event hit today, experts estimate it could knock out large portions of the power grid for weeks, months, or even years. Scientists estimate a 10–12% chance over the next century.

2. Nuclear Conflict (High-Altitude EMP)

Nuclear conflict has been a threat since World War II, but today there’s a lesser-known risk: high-altitude EMP.

A nuclear detonation high above Earth wouldn’t harm people directly, but it could disable power, communications, and electronics across an entire continent.

Studies surveying nuclear-risk experts estimate a 1–5% chance of a major nuclear-use event in the next few decades.

3. Pandemic

We just lived through COVID, so we don’t need to imagine this one. We saw empty shelves, supply shortages, and delays in essential services — even with the power still on.

Studies estimate a 1–3% chance each year of another major pandemic.

None of these scenarios are likely to happen tomorrow — but they’re not impossible.

Prepared, Not Paranoid

After watching that movie, something clicked. I thought:

I insure my truck.

I insure my house.

I even buy race insurance.

What insurance is there for a survival situation?!

The answer: none! There is no survival insurance. You are on you own when shit goes down.

Sooo I became a prepper — not a doomsday prepper with a bunker in the backyard like you would see on the history channel — just a moderately prepared kind of prepper.

Here's what I put together:

  • Emergency Food Ration Bars
  • Emergency Drinking Water Rations - Case of 64
  • Aquatabs for 1 L - (50 / pkg)
  • Lightsticks
  • 36 Hour Candle
  • WINDPROOF & Waterproof Storm Matches
  • Camp Heat - For Folding Stove
  • All Weather Emergency Heat Retention Bag
  • Fire Disc Fire Starter
  • Plastic Whistle
  • Rain & Emergency Poncho
  • Tube Tent
  • 124 Piece First Aid Kit
  • And yes, toilet paper

When we talk about rare, unpredictable disasters, the goal isn’t to panic, it’s to prepare.

It’s not about building a bunker. It’s not about being paranoid. It’s about being mindful of how fragile our systems really are and being prepared for the worst.

Because if the day ever comes when the lights go out and they don’t turn back on… you’ll wish you had more than noodles and blankets.
